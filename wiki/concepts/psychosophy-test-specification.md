# Psychosophy Test Specification

This page defines the first PsyCalc draft test for Psychosophy. It can learn structurally from known Psychosophy questionnaires and methods, but its items should remain original.

## Status

**Draft / ready for expert review, not validated.**

The output is a working hypothesis about a Psychosophy profile, not a final type verdict.

## Construct Frame

In PsyCalc, Psychosophy is treated as a heuristic model of **latent processes of synthesis and analysis in action**.

The test measures how a person organizes action across four domains:

- **Воля / Will (V)** — choosing direction, holding agency, deciding who defines the course;
- **Логика / Logic (L)** — argumentation, criteria, explanation, model-building;
- **Эмоция / Emotion (E)** — affective expression, atmosphere, emotional regulation;
- **Физика / Physics (F)** — body, comfort, resources, material routines.

## Position Axes

For each aspect, estimate three axes:

| Axis | Pole A | Pole B |
|---|---|---|
| Authoring | I set the frame | I receive the frame |
| Process | I need joint elaboration | I want a finished result |
| Adaptation | I adapt to another’s state | I insist on my own state |

Derived position hypotheses:

- **1st position** = authoring + result + self-insistence;
- **2nd position** = authoring + process + adaptation;
- **3rd position** = receptive + process + self-insistence;
- **4th position** = receptive + result + adaptation.

## Recommended MVP Structure

- 80–120 total items for a full pilot bank.
- 48–64 Likert items.
- 12–16 scenario items.
- 8–12 forced-choice pairs.
- 4–8 ranking tasks about priorities, delegation, and protected zones.

Use a 6-point Likert scale without a neutral midpoint for regular items.

## Seed Item Bank

These are original seed items for review. They are not a complete validated test.

### Scenario Items

1. When a group cannot choose a direction, I usually:
   - A. propose a decision and prefer moving forward; `V: 1-like`
   - B. help the group reach a decision without forcing mine; `V: 2-like`
   - C. keep checking whether I have the right to press; `V: 3-like`
   - D. prefer someone else to set the direction so I can join. `V: 4-like`

2. When a practical decision needs to be made, I prefer to:
   - A. build a workable explanation quickly and close the question; `L: 1-like`
   - B. discuss criteria until the reasoning is clear to everyone; `L: 2-like`
   - C. keep rechecking whether something important was missed; `L: 3-like`
   - D. use a clear scheme from someone competent. `L: 4-like`

3. In an emotionally tense conversation, I usually:
   - A. set the tone myself; `E: 1-like`
   - B. tune the atmosphere to the other person; `E: 2-like`
   - C. watch my expression carefully so it is not misunderstood; `E: 3-like`
   - D. join more easily once someone else creates a clear tone. `E: 4-like`

4. When daily routines or comfort are poor, I usually:
   - A. quickly reshape them to my standard; `F: 1-like`
   - B. discuss how to make things comfortable for everyone; `F: 2-like`
   - C. notice many discomforts but struggle to stabilize the solution; `F: 3-like`
   - D. can live with the existing setup if it basically works. `F: 4-like`

### Likert Items

5. I find it hard to relax when I do not know who has the right to decide. `V-3 signal`
6. I can help another person clarify their decision without needing it to become my decision. `V-2 signal`
7. If an explanation is inconsistent, I keep returning to it even when others want to move on. `L salience`
8. I prefer a concise ready-made explanation when the topic is not central to me. `L-4 signal`
9. Emotional ambiguity bothers me more when I cannot tell how my own expression is being received. `E-3 signal`
10. I often regulate the room’s emotional tone without thinking of it as a big performance. `E-2 signal`
11. I have clear standards for comfort, food, money, or physical rhythm, and I notice when they are crossed. `F-1 signal`
12. I can postpone practical comfort for a long time if another priority is more important. `F-4 signal`

### Forced-Choice Items

13. Which is more typical?
    - A. I prefer to close a decision once the direction is clear. `result`
    - B. I prefer to keep the decision open until the process feels internally honest. `process`

14. Which is more difficult?
    - A. Having my chosen direction broken by others. `1/3 self-insistence`
    - B. Having to be the main initiator when someone else could do it. `4 receptive`

15. In reasoning, I more often want:
    - A. my own clear conclusion; `1-like`
    - B. a shared thinking process; `2-like`
    - C. reassurance that I did not miss something; `3-like`
    - D. a usable answer from a trusted source. `4-like`

16. In material matters, I more often:
    - A. set a standard; `1-like`
    - B. coordinate standards with others; `2-like`
    - C. worry about getting the standard wrong; `3-like`
    - D. accept the available standard. `4-like`

## Scoring Sketch

1. Score each aspect on authoring, process/result, self-insistence/adaptation, pain, and low-priority yielding.
2. For each aspect, estimate probabilities for positions 1–4.
3. Assemble the most coherent permutation of V/L/E/F.
4. Return top 3 type hypotheses, contradictory signals, and suggested interview checks.

## Caveats

- Distinguishing 3rd vs 4th positions is difficult in self-report.
- Knowledge of Psychosophy can produce self-typing bias.
- Social desirability especially affects Will, Logic, and Emotion.
- Do not use this score for high-stakes decisions without interview, observation, and validation.

See [[typology-test-design-protocol]] for shared psychometric safeguards.
