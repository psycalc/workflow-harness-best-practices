# Complete Typology Reference: Three Levels of Personality Analysis

This document provides comprehensive descriptions of the three typological systems used in the Cognitive Matchmaker project: Temporistics (strategic level), Psychosophy (operational level), and Socionics (tactical level). Each typology is described through the lens of latent processes—the hidden internal mechanisms that manifest through observable behavior.

---

## Mapping Summary

| Typology | Level | Latent Process | Frame Type | Primary Focus |
|----------|-------|----------------|------------|---------------|
| Temporistics | Strategic | Inductive-deductive structuring of temporal experience | Temporal frame | Past, Present, Future, Eternity |
| Psychosophy | Operational | Analysis, synthesis, action organization | Action frame | Logic, Emotion, Will, Physics |
| Socionics | Tactical | Information modeling | Information frame | 8 aspects of information metabolism |

## Part I: Temporistics — Strategic Level

### What is Temporistics?

Temporistics describes how a person structures their relationship with time. Unlike simple "time orientation" (past/present/future focus), temporistics examines the **latent process** of how temporal experience is organized, integrated, and interpreted.

The key insight: a person doesn't just "prefer" the past or future—they have a characteristic way of deriving meaning from temporal material, of building their temporal identity, and of relating to time horizons.

### The Four Temporal Aspects

Temporistics identifies four fundamental aspects of temporal experience:

1. **Past** — How experience is processed, interpreted, and integrated into identity
2. **Present** — How the immediate situation is perceived and navigated
3. **Future** — How trajectories are projected, goals are formed, and uncertainty is managed
4. **Eternity** — How meaning, purpose, and existential continuity are constructed

### The Four Positions (How Each Aspect is Processed)

Within each aspect, the position describes the **mode of processing**:

| Position | Mode | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| 1st | Autonomous generation | Person sets the frame themselves, derives conclusions from internal thesis |
| 2nd | Cooperative modification | Person develops the frame in dialogue with others |
| 3rd | Reconstruction under deficit | Person agonizingly derives frame from problematic signals |
| 4th | External acceptance | Person more willingly takes the frame from another |

---

### Past — Processing of Experience

The Past aspect describes how a person relates to their history, how they interpret experience, and how their sense of identity is formed through accumulated experience.

#### 1st Past — Deductive from Self-Concept

**Latent process:** Person first holds an internal thesis about themselves, then reads the past through it. The general conclusion precedes the specific instances.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- Has a stable sense of identity that colors interpretation of past events
- Past events are selected and framed to confirm who they are
- Setbacks are reframed as character demonstrations
- Identity is seen as fixed and confirmed by history

**Example:**
"I am a person who always rises after defeats. Yes, I had failures, but they only proved my character."

**In relationships:** May have difficulty updating self-concept based on new evidence. May interpret partner's past through a fixed lens.

**Compatibility signal:** If both have strong 1st Past, may have difficulty adapting to new shared history.

#### 2nd Past — Joint Processing

**Latent process:** Person flexibly processes experience through dialogue and comparison with others. Identity is developed through mutual reflection.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- Values shared memories and mutual understanding
- "Let's look at your experience and mine, compare, understand what follows"
- Less rigid about self-narrative, more exploratory
- Identity emerges through relationship with others' perspectives

**Example:**
"Let's look at what happened in your similar situation, and compare with mine. What did we each learn?"

**In relationships:** Natural co-processing of relational history. Can update shared narrative together.

#### 3rd Past — Inductive from Painful Episodes

**Latent process:** Person collects particular experiences, especially painful ones, and derives heavy conclusions about self from them. The instances precede the conclusion.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- Past episodes often processed with guilt, regret, or self-criticism
- Tends to derive global conclusions from specific failures
- May dwell on negative patterns from history
- Identity often formed through what went wrong

**Example:**
"Then I was silent. Then I stepped back again. Then I didn't push an important decision. Probably, I'm weak and break at critical moments."

**In relationships:** May carry significant past relationship wounds. May interpret current situations through lens of past failures.

**Compatibility signal:** Needs partner who can help reframe without minimizing. May clash with 1st Past who doesn't understand the weight.

#### 4th Past — External Frame Acceptance

**Latent process:** Person less wants to build a strong concept of the past themselves and more easily takes a ready frame from others.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Tell me how it was and what's important to remember"
- Less invested in building comprehensive self-narrative
- May accept others' interpretation of shared history
- Less tendency to self-define through past

**Example:**
"I don't need to figure out what it means about me. My family has always seen me this way, and that's enough."

**In relationships:** May look to partner or community for interpretation of their shared history. Less likely to initiate deep historical processing.

### Present — Navigation of Immediate Situation

The Present aspect describes how a person perceives and acts within their immediate environment, how they understand their place in space and situation.

#### 1st Present — Deductive from Place-Sense

**Latent process:** Person first has an internal sense of their right place, then acts in space from that conviction.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "This is my territory"
- Clear sense of how things should be arranged
- Tendency to organize chaos
- Feels authorized to set the frame for the immediate situation

**Example:**
"This is my territory. I understand how things should be arranged here. If there's chaos here, it needs to be put in order."

**In relationships:** May naturally take leadership in shared spaces. May feel unsettled when their sense of place is challenged.

#### 2nd Present — Flexible Integration

**Latent process:** Person integrates into space through dialogue and mutual adaptation. The frame is developed cooperatively.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Let's together understand how to arrange things here"
- Flexible, adaptive to others' needs
- Creates shared space through negotiation
- Less territorial pressure

**Example:**
"Let's see what would be comfortable for both of us here, and how we can arrange things together."

**In relationships:** Natural co-creator of shared domestic/social space. Flexible about territory.

#### 3rd Present — Inductive from Environment

**Latent process:** Person collects environment signals and from them derives their position—often with uncertainty about their place.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- Reads the room extensively before acting
- May feel like an outsider or not fully integrated
- Uncertainty about where they fit
- Reactive to environmental cues

**Example:**
"Here everything seems already distributed. I don't understand where my place is. It seems possible to integrate, but there's a feeling I'm redundant."

**In relationships:** May need reassurance about their place in partner's life. May struggle with assertiveness in shared spaces.

#### 4th Present — External Frame-Taking

**Latent process:** Person takes orientation for the immediate situation from others. Less internal pressure about place.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Show me where to sit, where to go, how it's done here"
- Minimal own pressure on topic of place
- Willing to follow others' lead in immediate situation
- Adaptive rather than territorial

**Example:**
"I don't need to decide how we arrange the space. Just show me what works and I'll adapt."

**In relationships:** May need clear communication about their role in shared activities. Comfortable following partner's lead in unfamiliar situations.

---

### Future — Projection and Trajectory

The Future aspect describes how a person relates to what's coming, how they form expectations, set goals, and manage uncertainty about trajectories.

#### 1st Future — Deductive from Goal-Image

**Latent process:** Person first holds a clear image of the course/trajectory, then derives actions from it. The destination precedes the steps.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "It's clear to me where I'm going"
- Future is relatively determined
- Clear sense of right path vs wrong path
- Willing to make present sacrifices for future goals

**Example:**
"It's clear to me where I'm going. So today I need to refuse the unnecessary, this path is right, and that one is not."

**In relationships:** May have clear vision of relationship trajectory. May expect partner to align with their projected future.

#### 2nd Future — Joint Navigation

**Latent process:** Person develops trajectory through dialogue and exploration with others. Future is navigated collaboratively.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Let's consider options and choose a route together"
- Flexible about specific destination
- Values joint decision-making about direction
- Future is a shared project

**Example:**
"Let's look at the options together and decide what trajectory works for both of us."

**In relationships:** Natural partner in long-term planning. Values joint vision of future.

#### 3rd Future — Inductive from Risks

**Latent process:** Person collects risks, uncertainties, failures (real and imagined) and derives anxious conclusions about trajectory.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- Future often imagined through potential problems
- Difficulty maintaining positive future image
- May catastrophize possible outcomes
- Focus on what could go wrong

**Example:**
"The economy is unstable, people are unpredictable, I've already made wrong choices, and I'm still not confident in myself. It seems I might go wrong again."

**In relationships:** May need support in maintaining positive relationship vision. May express anxiety about future trajectory.

**Compatibility signal:** Needs partner who can hold positive future image without dismissing concerns.

#### 4th Future — External Frame-Taking

**Latent process:** Person more easily takes future direction from others or from external sources. Less internal drive to project trajectory.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "If there's a reliable person or clear course, I'll calmly follow it"
- Future is easier taken from outside than formulated from inside
- Willing to defer to trusted others
- Less anxious when direction is provided

**Example:**
"I don't need to figure out the whole plan. If you know where we're going, I'll follow."

**In relationships:** May need partner with strong future vision. Comfortable when direction is clear.

---

### Eternity — Meaning and Existential Continuity

The Eternity aspect describes how a person relates to questions of meaning, purpose, and existential continuity beyond the immediate temporal frame.

#### 1st Eternity — Deductive from Meta-Meaning

**Latent process:** Person starts from a big meaning, principle, or frame, and reads life through it. The overarching narrative precedes specific experience.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Life is not random, everything has a purpose"
- Strong existential framework
- Trials and suffering integrated into larger meaning
- Life is seen as having structure and purpose

**Example:**
"Life is not random. Everything has a purpose. So my trials also have a place in the general picture."

**In relationships:** May have strong views on relationship meaning and purpose. May see relationship as part of larger life meaning.

#### 2nd Eternity — Dialogue on Meaning

**Latent process:** Person develops their existential framework through dialogue, comparison of worldviews, and joint philosophizing.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Let's discuss, compare worldviews, search for meaning"
- Open to others' perspectives on existential questions
- Meaning emerges through relationship and dialogue
- Less attached to fixed metaphysical framework

**Example:**
"I want to explore the meaning of this together, compare our perspectives, see what emerges."

**In relationships:** Natural partner for deep philosophical discussions. Values shared existential exploration.

#### 3rd Eternity — Agonizing Induction from Experience

**Latent process:** Person tries to derive meaning from fragments of experience, pain, and life pieces—but the derivation is anxious and unstable.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- Deep existential questions without stable answers
- May see mostly meaninglessness and suffering
- Difficulty integrating pain into coherent meaning structure
- Large gap between existential questions and available answers

**Example:**
"People suffer, die, make mistakes, much is meaningless. Then what is the meaning of my life specifically? Does it even exist?"

**In relationships:** May struggle with meaning questions in relationship context. May need partner who can hold meaning without forcing premature closure.

#### 4th Eternity — External System Acceptance

**Latent process:** Person more easily accepts existing meaning systems, traditions, or teachers rather than creating metaphysics independently.

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "It's easier for me to rely on an already existing system of meaning, tradition, teacher, faith"
- Not so much create metaphysics yourself, as accept and learn
- Stable existential framework through external authority
- Less existential anxiety when framework is provided

**Example:**
"I don't need to figure out the meaning of everything myself. Following the tradition/teacher/faith gives me what I need."

**In relationships:** May need partner with clear value/meaning framework. Comfortable when relational meaning is provided by tradition or shared faith.

### Temporal Type Formulas (Strategic Typological Positions)

A person's temporal type is determined by their position in each aspect:

| Position in Aspect | General Pattern |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| 1st | "I set the frame myself" |
| 2nd | "I develop the frame in dialogue" |
| 3rd | "I agonizingly derive the frame from problematic signals" |
| 4th | "I more willingly take the frame from another" |

**For Past:**
- 1P: "I already understand who I am, and the past confirms this"
- 3P: "I look at past episodes and from them derive who I am, often painfully"

---

### Strategic Compatibility Patterns

**Possible strategic compatibility indicators in the current project model:**

- Complementary positions in shared temporal aspects (1st + 4th, 2nd + 2nd)
- Shared approach to temporal processing (both deductive or both comfortable with ambiguity)
- Ability to hold each other's temporal narratives without dismissal

**Strategic conflict indicators:**

- 1st Past vs 3rd Past: One sees past as confirmation, other as source of self-criticism
- 1st Future vs 3rd Future: One has clear trajectory, other sees only risks
- Competing meaning systems without dialogue space
- Hard incompatibility on life milestones (children, career path, lifestyle)

**Potentially smoother combinations:**
- 1st + 4th in same aspect: One generates frame, other accepts it without struggle
- 2nd + 2nd: Joint processing of temporal material
- 1st + 3rd with mutual understanding: One can hold stable narrative while helping other process

**Challenging combinations:**
- 1st + 1st: Both want to set the temporal frame
- 3rd + 3rd: Both agonizing over temporal meaning without resolution
- 1st + 3rd: One doesn't understand why the other can't just move forward

## Part II: Psychosophy — Operational Level

### What is Psychosophy?

Psychosophy describes how a person works with tasks, goals, methods of action, and role distribution. While the surface labels are "Logic," "Emotion," "Will," and "Physics" (material), the deeper focus is on the **latent processes of analysis and synthesis**: how a person decomposes a situation and assembles a working holistic scheme of action.

### The Four Aspects

Psychosophy identifies four aspects of action organization:

1. **Logic (L)** — Working with truth, correctness, structure, systems
2. **Emotion (E)** — Working with feelings, atmosphere, relationships, expressions
3. **Will (W)** — Working with decisions, commitment, force, control, initiative
4. **Physics (F)** — Working with material, resources, comfort, routine, money

### The Four Positions (Analysis and Synthesis Modes)

For each aspect, the position describes the characteristic mode of analysis (decomposing) and synthesis (assembling):

| Position | Analysis Mode | Synthesis Mode | Result |
|----------|--------------|----------------|--------|
| 1st | Confident self-analysis | Self-assembles complete solution | Gives ready working contour |
| 2nd | Flexible joint analysis | Cooperative synthesis | Adapts solution for both |
| 3rd | Stuck, rechecks, doubts | Poorly glues into stable whole | Anxious, prolonged processing |
| 4th | Minimal self-analysis | Takes ready frame | Acts within externally given scheme |

---

### Logic Through Synthesis/Analysis

Logic describes how a person works with truth, correctness, systems, and structures. The latent process is about how they build and communicate models of how things work.

#### 1L — Primary Modeling

**Analysis:** Quickly decomposes situation into logical components
**Synthesis:** Self-assembles complete working model

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Look, here are 3 banks, 2 types of rates, hidden commissions, here's the income risk"
- "So the optimal scheme is this. We take option B"
- Natural confidence in own logical constructs
- Others can rely on their structural understanding

**Example (mortgage decision):**
"One sees: 3 banks, 2 rate types, hidden fees, income risk. Concludes: option B is optimal."

**In relationships:** May naturally take lead on logical planning. May not understand why others can't see the obvious structure.

#### 2L — Cooperative Modeling

**Analysis:** Parses criteria together with partner
**Synthesis:** Assembles solution that balances both perspectives

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Let's look together. You worry more about the monthly payment, I worry about overpayment. OK, let's divide criteria"
- Flexible, adaptive
- Values joint logical understanding
- Creates shared models

**Example:**
"Let's divide the criteria and find what works for both of us."

**In relationships:** Natural partner for joint planning and problem-solving. Values collaborative logical process.

#### 3L — Anxious Modeling

**Analysis:** Stuck in doubts, rechecks excessively
**Synthesis:** Cannot assemble final scheme due to persistent uncertainty

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Wait, what if we didn't account for insurance? What if I misunderstood the terms? What if the conclusion is completely wrong?"
- Excessive doubt about own logical constructs
- Difficulty reaching conclusions
- May consult many sources without resolving

**Example:**
"Can we really make this decision? What about X, what about Y, what if Z happens?"

**In relationships:** May need support in reaching logical conclusions. May second-guess decisions. Partner may need to provide certainty.

#### 4L — Frame-Taking

**Analysis:** Minimal independent analysis
**Synthesis:** Accepts pre-assembled model if trusted

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Explain briefly what's the difference"
- "OK, understood. If you checked it, we take this option"
- Doesn't build complex model independently
- Trusts others' logical frameworks

**Example:**
"You already checked the logic? OK, I trust your conclusion."

**In relationships:** May need partner with clear logical framework. Will follow if trust is established. May struggle with being put in position of primary logical responsibility.

---

### Emotion Through Synthesis/Analysis

Emotion describes how a person works with feelings, atmosphere, interpersonal dynamics, and emotional expression. The latent process is about how they map and shape the emotional fabric of situations.

#### 1E — Primary Emotional Modeling

**Analysis:** Quickly identifies emotional dynamics at play
**Synthesis:** Assembles emotional frame and leads its shaping

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Now the problem is not in facts but in coldness and alienation"
- "Need to first return warmth, then discuss"
- Natural sense of emotional architecture
- Comfortable leading emotional climate

**Example (after a quarrel):**
"One sees: it's about coldness and distance, not the facts. Conclusion: restore warmth first."

**In relationships:** May naturally sense emotional undercurrents. May lead relationship emotional climate. May not understand why others miss obvious emotional dynamics.

#### 2E — Cooperative Emotional Shaping

**Analysis:** Identifies emotional dynamics in interaction with partner
**Synthesis:** Jointly constructs emotional solution

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "You're closing off, I'm nervous, so first need to reduce tension"
- "Let me say it softer, and you answer without defense—conversation will work"
- Flexible, adaptive emotional approach
- Creates shared emotional space

**Example:**
"Let's both soften our approach so the conversation can actually happen."

**In relationships:** Natural co-regulator of relationship emotional climate. Values collaborative emotional processing.

#### 3E — Anxious Emotional Mapping

**Analysis:** Uncertain what they're feeling, how to express it
**Synthesis:** Assembles emotional whole with difficulty, lots of constraint

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "I don't understand what I'm feeling now and how to express it correctly. What if they misunderstand me?"
- May suppress or misinterpret own emotions
- Difficulty reaching emotional clarity
- May over-analyze others' emotions

**Example:**
"I think I'm angry? Or maybe I'm hurt? I can't tell, and I don't know how to bring it up."

**In relationships:** May need significant support in emotional communication. May appear to suppress emotions until overflow. Partner may need to help decode and create safe emotional expression space.

#### 4E — Frame-Taking

**Analysis:** Minimal independent emotional analysis
**Synthesis:** Adapts to emotional tone set by others

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Please, no extra drama"
- "If you set a calm tone, I'll enter into it"
- Doesn't want to be primary emotional architect
- Comfortable following others' emotional lead

**Example:**
"You set the mood for the gathering, I'll adapt to it."

**In relationships:** May need partner with emotional clarity. May struggle if expected to lead emotional processing. Will respond well to calm, clear emotional framing.

---

### Will Through Synthesis/Analysis

Will describes how a person works with decisions, commitment, control, initiative, and volitional action. The latent process is about how they assemble and execute volitional courses of action.

#### 1W — Primary Volitional Assembly

**Analysis:** Quickly decomposes decision into factors and priorities
**Synthesis:** Self-assembles volitional plan with clear commitment

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "There are 4 factors: work, money, family, deadlines. Work is most important, deadline is hard"
- "Plan is: in 2 months move, you close housing, I close work"
- Comfortable with volitional decisions
- Natural leadership and initiative

**Example (relocation decision):**
"Four factors matter: work, money, family, deadlines. Priority is work. Plan: I handle job search, partner handles housing."

**In relationships:** May naturally take initiative in decisions. May need to watch for taking too much volitional control.

#### 2W — Cooperative Volitional Planning

**Analysis:** Jointly explores fears and priorities
**Synthesis:** Together assembles acceptable course

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Let's understand what scares you most about moving, and what's critical for me"
- "Then the general course is: first a trial period for 3 months, then a decision"
- Flexible about who takes initiative
- Creates shared volitional space

**Example:**
"Let's first understand both our concerns, then build a plan that works for both."

**In relationships:** Natural co-decision makers. Values joint commitment process. May share initiative naturally.

#### 3W — Anxious Volitional Assembly

**Analysis:** Stuck in competing pressures and rights
**Synthesis:** Difficulty assembling stable line of action

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "If I give in—they'll push me. If I press—there will be conflict. What if I don't even have the right to decide?"
- Volitional topic is painful
- Difficulty asserting own will
- May swing between submission and aggression

**Example:**
"I don't know if I even have the right to push for this, or if I should just give in..."

**In relationships:** May need support in asserting needs. May appear passive or aggressive inconsistently. Partner may need to help create safe space for volitional expression.

#### 4W — Frame-Taking

**Analysis:** Minimal independent volitional analysis
**Synthesis:** Follows course if provided by trusted other

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "What options do we have?"
- "If you've already thought through the route, I can calmly join"
- Doesn't like being primary initiator
- Comfortable following clear direction

**Example:**
"You figured out the plan? Great, I'll support and follow."

**In relationships:** May need partner with clear volitional direction. May struggle if forced into primary initiative role. Will commit strongly once plan is clear.

---

### Physics (Material) Through Synthesis/Analysis

Physics describes how a person works with material organization: household, money, resources, comfort, and routine. The latent process is about how they assemble and maintain material systems.

#### 1F — Primary Material Organization

**Analysis:** Quickly sees material structure and needs
**Synthesis:** Self-assembles household/financial system

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Here are mandatory expenses, here's the reserve, here's the nonsense we shouldn't spend on"
- "The financial scheme of the family should be like this"
- Natural material organizer
- Comfortable setting material standards

**Example (family budget):**
"Three categories: essentials, savings, discretionary. Here's the monthly allocation."

**In relationships:** May naturally manage household logistics. May take lead on financial planning. May need to check if partner wants input.

#### 2F — Cooperative Material Organization

**Analysis:** Jointly explores material needs and comfort
**Synthesis:** Assembles system balanced for both

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Let's look at what you really need for comfort, and what I need"
- "Let's assemble such an expense routine that it's comfortable for both"
- Flexible about material arrangements
- Creates shared material framework

**Example:**
"Let's figure out our comfort needs together and build a budget that works for both."

**In relationships:** Natural co-managers of household and finances. Values collaborative material decisions.

#### 3F — Anxious Material Processing

**Analysis:** Worried about adequacy, judgment, appropriateness
**Synthesis:** Difficulty assembling stable household solution

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Are we really enough? Won't it look too poor? Am I too demanding?"
- Material topic causes anxiety
- May oscillate between extremes
- Difficulty establishing stable material routine

**Example:**
"Are we being too cheap? What will people think? But maybe we're spending too much?"

**In relationships:** May need reassurance about material decisions. May appear to fluctuate in material expectations. Partner may need to help establish stable material framework.

#### 4F — Frame-Taking

**Analysis:** Minimal independent material analysis
**Synthesis:** Adapts to established material system

**Characteristic behavior:**
- "Tell me what's the basic normal option"
- "If this is a working household scheme, I'll adapt"
- Doesn't want to be primary material architect
- Comfortable with established material systems

**Example:**
"What's the normal budget for this? If it's reasonable, I'll work with it."

**In relationships:** May need partner with clear material vision. Will integrate well into established household systems. May struggle if expected to lead material organization.

### Psychosophic Type Formulas (Operational Typological Positions)

A person's psychosophic type is determined by their position in each aspect:

| Position | Analysis | Synthesis | In Relationships |
|----------|----------|-----------|------------------|
| 1st | "Quickly broke down" | "Quickly assembled" | "Gives ready working contour" |
| 2nd | "Parsed together" | "Assembled together" | "Adapted for two" |
| 3rd | "Analyzes too long" | "Doubts, poorly glues" | "Stuck, needs support" |
| 4th | "Minimally analyzes" | "Takes already assembled" | "Acts within frame" |

### Operational Compatibility Patterns

**Potentially smoother combinations:**

- **1 + 4** in same aspect: One assembles the scheme, other calmly accepts it
  - Example: 1L + 4L: One explains structure, other trusts and follows
  - Example: 1E + 4E: One sets emotional tone, other enters it comfortably
  
- **2 + 3** in same aspect: One helps parse and assemble without pressure
  - Example: 2L + 3L: Partner helps 3L reach conclusion without overwhelming
  - Example: 2E + 3E: Partner helps 3E decode emotions in safe space

- **2 + 2**: Both naturally co-process
  - Both comfortable with joint analysis and synthesis
  - Natural collaboration on decisions

**Challenging combinations:**

- **1 + 1**: Both want to be the primary scheme-builder
  - Competition for logical/emotional/volitional leadership
  - May argue about whose framework is correct
  
- **1 + 3**: One already assembled, other still in analysis
  - 1 may push 3 to conclude before ready
  - 3 may feel pressured by 1's speed
  
- **3 + 3**: Both stuck in analysis
  - May reinforce each other's doubts
  - Difficulty reaching conclusions without external help
  
- **4 + 4**: Both wait for external framework
  - May not generate necessary structure
  - May struggle when no external authority provided

**Compatibility formula:**
Operational compatibility = compatibility of synthesis/analysis methods in action

## Part III: Socionics — Tactical Level

### What is Socionics?

Socionics describes how a person builds an internal information map of situations and relates to information aspects. While it describes 8 functions and 16 types, the deeper focus is on the **latent process of information modeling**: how a person perceives, interprets, and communicates about the world.

### The Eight Information Aspects

Socionics identifies eight aspects of information:

| Aspect | Name | Focus |
|--------|------|-------|
| Ti | Structural Logic | Truth, structure, consistency, classification |
| Te | Decision Logic | Efficiency, pragmatism, resource management |
| Fi | Ethics of Relations | Warmth, closeness, harmony, values |
| Fe | Ethics of Emotions | Expression, atmosphere, influence, persuasion |
| Si | Sensing Comfort | Comfort, body, sensation, wellbeing |
| Se | Sensing Will | Power, control, influence, environment |
| Ni | Intuition of Time | Future projections, development, meaning |
| Ne | Intuition of Ideas | Possibilities, creativity, abstractions |

### Model A: The Eight Functions

Each type has 8 functions, each working with one aspect:

| Position | Name | General Description |
|----------|------|---------------------|
| 1 | Basic (Ego) | Main way of modeling reality, strongest and most natural |
| 2 | Creative (Ego) | Produces new based on what basic accepts, flexible application |
| 3 | Role | Forced social modeling, situational acceptance under demand |
| 4 | Vulnerable | Zone where map most often tears, weak and painful |
| 5 | Suggestive | Unconscious search for corresponding information from outside |
| 6 | Activation | Energized when gently developed by appropriate partner |
| 7 | Ignoring | Strong but not valued, can use but doesn't center on it |
| 8 | Demonstrative | Background, automatic, casual application |

---

### How Functions Work as Latent Processes

#### 1st Function — Primary Modeling

**Latent process:** I myself build the main map of reality

**Characteristic:**
- Fastest, most confident information processing in this aspect
- Person considers it natural and obvious
- Can explain and defend conclusions
- Others often recognize them as expert in this area

**Example (1Ti):**
Person quickly builds internal scheme: "Here's where the input is, here's the bottleneck, here's where role structure is broken, here's why decisions are stuck."

#### 2nd Function — Creative Modeling

**Latent process:** Flexibly fine-tune the model to the situation

**Characteristic:**
- Produces new based on what basic accepts
- More situational, task-oriented
- Can rebuild model for current needs
- Less rigid than basic, more adaptive

**Example (2Ti):**
"Let's rebuild the model for the current task. If we remove one level of coordination and differently decompose roles, the system will move faster."

#### 3rd Function — Role Modeling

**Latent process:** Trying to model "as needed," but uncertainly and situationally

**Characteristic:**
- Modeling because "that's how it should be"
- Internal map held tensely
- Under social demand, may force capability
- Unnatural, effortful application

**Example (3Ti):**
Person tries to look structural and collected: "Yes, yes, let's fix the rules, formalize the scheme," but internally uncertain.

#### 4th Function — Vulnerable Modeling

**Latent process:** Here the map most often tears

**Characteristic:**
- Information processing breaks down under pressure
- Painful topic, avoided if possible
- Can understand fragments but not stable modeling
- Under stress, may completely lose capability

**Example (4Ti):**
"Don't pressure me with a rigid scheme, I get lost when everything needs to be formalized without gaps."

#### 5th Function — Suggestive Seeking

**Latent process:** I want to be given the missing way of seeing

**Characteristic:**
- Doesn't want to be main author of model
- Values when partner calmly provides missing structure
- Unconsciously searches for this information from outside
- Feels enhanced when appropriate information is provided

**Example (5Ti):**
"Explain to me how it's arranged, give me a working scheme, and I'll feel better."

#### 6th Function — Activation

**Latent process:** I come alive when I'm gently developed in this aspect

**Characteristic:**
- Doesn't always immediately build model
- Energized when gently drawn into it
- Responds to support and acceleration
- Can develop rapidly with appropriate partner

**Example (6Ti):**
"Look, if we decompose like this, you yourself can continue." Mobilizes when gently supported.

#### 7th Function — Ignoring

**Latent process:** I can do this, but don't consider it main

**Characteristic:**
- Often sees the information quite strongly
- Doesn't want to make it the center
- Can use if needed, doesn't live in it
- Will apply if it serves purpose but won't prioritize

**Example (7Ti):**
"Yes, the scheme is clear, but let's get to the point already."

#### 8th Function — Demonstrative

**Latent process:** I do this in the background, almost without noticing

**Characteristic:**
- Casual, automatic application
- No special emphasis or effort
- Almost unconscious competence
- Provides background support without being focal

**Example (8Ti):**
"I slightly rearranged roles and cut steps here, now everything works."

### Compatibility Through Model A

The most natural support in Model A goes where:
- Basic function of one hits Suggestive of another (1 → 5)
- Creative hits Activation (2 → 6)

This means: one person naturally gives the way of seeing that the second lacks, and the second doesn't need to fight for it.

#### Duality (Classic Socionics Best-Fit Hypothesis)

**Pattern:** Functions mirror: 1↔5, 2↔6, 3↔7, 4↔8

**In practice:**
- Each provides what the other unconsciously seeks
- 1st of one fills 5th need of other, and vice versa
- 2nd of one activates 6th of other, and vice versa
- No forcing on vulnerable functions
- Natural, energizing interaction

**Example (1Ti + 5Ti duality):**
One has 1Ti: they calmly build clear model of process.
Other has 5Ti: they lack exactly such a structural map.
First says: "Look, the problem is not in people, but in that roles and decision criteria are mixed."
Second internally relaxes: "Yes, this is exactly what I lacked."

#### Conflict (Most Challenging)

**Pattern:** 1 reflects into 4 of other, 2 into 3, etc.

**In practice:**
- One naturally gives exactly the type of information that for the second is weak and painful
- Constant pressure on vulnerable functions
- What one sees as helpful, other experiences as attack

**Example (1Ti + 4Ti conflict):**
One has 1Ti: "Everything should be clear, non-contradictory, and logically pure."
Other has 4Ti: excessive structural pressure causes constraint and breaking.
First thinks: "I'm just bringing order."
Second feels: "I'm being broken by schemes."

## Part IV: Cross-Level Integration

### How Levels Interact

The three levels are not independent—they interact:

1. **Strategic (Temporistics)** sets the overall direction and temporal frame
2. **Operational (Psychosophy)** organizes how actions are taken toward that direction
3. **Tactical (Socionics)** handles moment-to-moment information processing

**Example integration:**
- **Strategic:** "I have a long-term vision of building a family together" (1st Future)
- **Operational:** "We'll co-decide on major life decisions together" (2W)
- **Tactical:** "We naturally share our understanding of situations and support each other's development" (duality)

### Compatibility at Multiple Levels

In the current project model, stronger compatibility may require:
- Strategic: Shared temporal orientation and meaning framework
- Operational: Complementary approaches to action and decision-making
- Tactical: Natural information exchange and mutual support of modeling

**Mismatch at any level can create friction:**
- Strategic mismatch: Fundamentally different life directions
- Operational mismatch: Competing approaches to getting things done
- Tactical mismatch: Constant pressure on vulnerable functions
