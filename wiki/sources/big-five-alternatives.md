# Big Five Alternatives in Personality Psychology

> **Status:** Overview — This document catalogs main alternative trait models to the Five-Factor Model (Big Five).

---

## Overview: Why Alternatives Exist

The Big Five has dominated personality psychology since the 1990s, but many researchers argue that broad Five-Factor domains do not exhaust every useful personality construct.

1. **Dimensions not contained in Big Five** — new factors (e.g., HEXACO's Honesty-Humility)
2. **Predictive advantages** — narrow traits predict outcomes better in specific domains

---

## Major Alternative Models

### HEXACO (Six-Factor Model)

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Factors** | 6 (H, E, X, A, C, O) |
| **Authors** | Michael C. Ashton & Kibeom Lee |
| **Year** | 2004 |
| **Method** | Lexical approach across languages |

**The Six Factors:**

| Factor | Description |
|--------|------------|
| **H** (Honesty-Humility) | Sincerity, fairness, modesty, greed avoidance |
| **E** (Emotionality) | Fearfulness, anxiety, sentimentality |
| **X** (eXtraversion) | Sociability, assertiveness, activity level |
| **A** (Agreeableness) | Forgivingness, gentleness, flexibility, patience |
| **C** (Conscientiousness) | Orderliness, self-discipline, achievement strivings |
| **O** (Openness to Experience) | Aesthetic appreciation, intellectual curiosity |

**What's NEW in HEXACO:**
- **Honesty-Humility** — not in Big Five
- **Emotionality** partly reorganizes material that overlaps with Big Five Neuroticism
- **Agreeableness** is structured differently from Big Five Agreeableness
- **Honesty-Humility** is the clearest added broad factor beyond Big Five

**Key Papers:**
```
Ashton, M.C. & Lee, K. (2007). Empirical, theoretical, and practical advantages 
of the HEXACO model. Personality and Social Psychology Review, 11(2), 150-166.
```

### 16PF (Cattell's Sixteen Personality Factors)

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Factors** | 16 primary + 5 global |
| **Author** | Raymond Cattell |
| **Year** | 1946 (16PF), 1994 (5th edition) |
| **Method** | Factor analysis of L-data, Q-data, T-data |

**Structure:**
- 16 primary factors (surface traits)
- 5 global (second-order) factors that overlap substantially with Big Five-style broad traits
- 2 third-order factors

**Primary Factors:**
```
A: Warmth, B: Reasoning, C: Emotional Stability,
E: Dominance, F: Liveliness, G: Rule-Consciousness,
H: Social Boldness, I: Sensitivity, L: Vigilance,
M: Abstractedness, N: Privateness, O: Apprehension,
Q1: Openness to Change, Q2: Self-Reliance, Q3: Perfectionism,
Q4: Tension
```

**Note:** Cattell's 16PF includes broader second-order factors that overlap strongly with Big Five dimensions, but they are not simply identical to the contemporary Big Five.

**Key Papers:**
```
Cattell, R.B., Eber, H.W. & Tatsuoka, M.M. (1970). Handbook for the 16PF.
```

### PEN Model (Eysenck)

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Factors** | 3 |
| **Author** | Hans J. Eysenck |
| **Year** | 1967 (book), 1975 (questionnaire) |
| **Basis** | Historically presented as biologically grounded |

**The Three Factors:**

| Factor | Description |
|--------|------------|
| **P** (Psychoticism) | Antisocial, tough-minded, impulsive |
| **E** (Extraversion) | Sociable, active, arousal-seeking |
| **N** (Neuroticism) | Anxious, depressed, emotionally unstable |

**Key Distinction:**
> "Psychoticism" ≠ psychosis — it's about **tough-mindedness** and rule-breaking

**Biological Basis:**
- Eysenck framed PEN as biologically grounded, especially for Extraversion and Neuroticism
- Some proposed physiological mechanisms became influential historically, but should not be treated as settled summaries of the whole model

**Key Papers:**
```
Eysenck, H.J. (1967). The Biological Basis of Personality.
Eysenck, S.B.G. & Eysenck, H.J. (1975). Manual of the Eysenck Personality Questionnaire.
```

### Reinforcement Sensitivity Theory (RST/Gray)

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Systems** | 3 |
| **Author** | Jeffrey Gray (revised by McNaughton) |
| **Year** | 1970s, revised 2000 |

**The Three Systems:**

| System | Behavior |
|--------|----------|
| **BIS** (Behavior Inhibition System) | Conflict monitoring and anxiety |
| **BAS** (Behavior Activation System) | Reward-seeking, impulsivity |
| **FFFS** (Fight-Flight-Freeze System) | Fear and avoidance of aversive stimuli |

**Relation to other models:**
- RST is a biopsychological motivational framework, not a direct replacement for the Big Five
- Comparisons with Eysenck are common historically, but the mapping is only approximate

## Narrow Trait Models

### Dark Tetrad

Four dark personality traits (expansion of Dark Triad):

| Trait | Description |
|-------|------------|
| **Machiavellianism** | Manipulative, strategic self-interest |
| **Narcissism** | Grandiosity, entitlement |
| **Psychopathy** | Callousness, impulsivity, antisocial |
| **Sadism** | Pleasure in inflicting pain (added) |

**Key finding:** Dark-trait measures can add predictive value for some antisocial or exploitative outcomes, but they also overlap substantially with low Agreeableness and especially low Honesty-Humility.

### Trait Emotional Intelligence (Trait EI)

| Author | Measure |
|--------|---------|
| K. V. Petrides | TEIQue |

**Components:**
- Well-being
- Self-emotion
- Sociability
- Emotionality

**Note:** Controversial — not always accepted as "intelligence"

### Which to Use for Validation

| Our System | Alternative for Validation | Notes |

| **Socionics** | Big Five + HEXACO | Best-established external trait benchmarks |
| **Psychosophy** | 16PF | Possible higher-resolution comparison space |

### Key Insight from Research

> Broad Big Five domains are useful, but narrower traits and alternative models may improve prediction or capture constructs that sit partly outside the standard Five-Factor space.

**Implication for Cognitive Matchmaker:**
- Use Big Five/HEXACO as validation baseline
- But recognize narrow traits may add predictive power for specific outcomes
