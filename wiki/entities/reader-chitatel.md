# Reader (Читатель)

For the "Reader," the past is a **resource**, fuel, a bargaining chip. They don't consider the past important—they ignore the search for the answer to "who am I" to leave themselves strength for answering other, more important questions in their personal philosophy. The "Reader" neither creates their history nor criticizes it.

## Characteristics

- Past is closely connected with self-identification
- "Readers" are often perceived as "dark horses" from the outside
- Can communicate with them for many years and never fully understand what kind of person they are
- The "Reader" themselves doesn't understand this

## Blind Spot

The Reader finds it difficult to use their "luggage," extract useful experience, understand whether they possess certain qualities or not. The Reader is, as it were, alienated from their past—it's not their book, and not their duty to write new chapters there.

At the same time, the reader gladly interacts with a friendly Author, allows them to use their qualities, skills, acquaintances—this allows the Reader's past to more fully reveal its true purpose, since the Reader (consciously or not) sacrifices their past for something more important, and it's important to them that the sacrifice was not in vain.

## Famous Example

From "Winnie-the-Pooh and All, All" by A. Latyshev, Winnie-the-Pooh (Reader):

The blind aspect of Pooh is indicated from the very first chapter. Pooh lived in the forest all alone, under the name Sanders.

*"What does 'lived under a name' mean?"—Christopher Robin immediately asked.*

*"It means that on a little board above the door there were golden letters saying 'Mr. Sanders', and he lived under it."*

This little remark about our hero's life has symbolic meaning—it speaks about the Reader's ability to perceive themselves, their qualities, their history without evaluation. The "Reader" simply lives next to their past, just as Pooh simply lives under the sign "Sanders."

### Key Episodes

- Pooh doesn't recognize his own tracks: "Sometimes like it's him, sometimes like it's not him. Can you guess from tracks?"

- Pooh doesn't recognize his own house: "Pooh—this is your own house!" "Ah, yes, right! Then let's go in!"
